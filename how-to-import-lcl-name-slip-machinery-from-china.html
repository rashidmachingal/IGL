1. Understanding LCL Imports for Name Slip Machinerys

Less-than-Container Load (LCL) refers to shipments that occupy only a portion of a full container. Rather than booking and paying for an entire 20′ or 40′ container, you share space with other exporters’ cargo. For capital-intensive machinery like the Name Slip Machinery—often weighing hundreds of kilograms but not filling a container—LCL can dramatically reduce freight costs.

Benefits of LCL for Name Slip Machinery

  • Cost-Efficiency: You pay only for the cubic meters (CBM) your machine occupies, not unused space.
  • Flexibility: Smaller consignments can ship more frequently, reducing lead times and working capital tied up in inventory.
  • Scalability: Ideal for first-time importers or companies testing new equipment without a large upfront shipping commitment.

3. Quality Control & Sample Approval

When importing precision equipment, you cannot rely solely on digital photos or spec sheets. Quality control (QC) and sample evaluation are non-negotiable.

Typical QC workflow:

  1. Pre-Production Sample: Supplier manufactures a prototype or sample unit and performs visual, dimensional, and functional tests against your specifications.
  2. In-Process Inspection (IPI): QC officer visits the factory when production reaches 50–70% to verify critical tolerances, material grades, and assembly procedures.
  3. Final Random Inspection (FRI): Checks finished machines for performance, safety, and cosmetic defects, running test batches of name slips to confirm cut quality and speed.

4. Consolidation & LCL Shipping Process

Once your machines pass QC, the next step is consolidating them with other shippers’ cargo into a single container at a bonded warehouse—this is the essence of LCL shipping.

LCL consolidation steps:

  1. Freight Booking: Freight forwarder reserves space on a scheduled vessel and provides you with an LCL rate based on origin, destination, and CBM.
  2. Pickup & Inland Haulage: IGL arranges secure, GPS-tracked trucking from the supplier’s factory to the consolidation warehouse.
  3. Warehouse Handling: Cargo is checked for labeling, packaging integrity, and weight/volume accuracy. Each package receives a House Bill of Lading (HBL) number for traceability.
  4. Container Stuffing: Consolidator loads multiple shippers’ pallets into the container, securing and bracing cargo to prevent movement during transit.
  5. Export Customs Clearance: IGL files export declarations, commercial invoice, packing list, and certificate of origin to seal the container for departure.

6. Customs Clearance & Duties

  1. Arrival Notice: Carrier issues notice of arrival to consignee/freight forwarder.
  2. Document Submission: Submit all required documents and pay import duties, VAT/GST, and any safeguard duties.
  3. Physical Inspection (if triggered): Random or risk-based release for cargo inspection.
  4. Customs Release: Once duties are paid and inspection cleared, customs issues a release order.
  5. Delivery Order & Gate Pass: Obtain from carrier and terminal, then arrange trucking to your site.
